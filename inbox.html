Gilang Ilma

Helping Merchants Manage Orders in Peak-Hours

Company

GoTo (Gojek/GoFood Merchants)

Year

2023

Role

Product Designer

Team

PM, Engineers, UX Researcher, UX Writer

Overview

The order inbox is the most opened and used page in the GoFood merchant app, where merchants see, accept, and process every incoming order. Yet it had never been redesigned since it was first launched. What started as a simple screen showing menu, quantity, and price had accumulated 7 years of additions: variants, notes, cutlery options, driver status, and more.

Problem

The page became unscalable. With everything displayed at once on the order list, merchants had to scroll intensively and the screen felt cluttered. Accessibility issues compounded this: small fonts, poor color contrast, and no clear information hierarchy made it difficult to read and comprehend order details, especially when multiple orders arrived simultaneously.

Solution

We simplified the order list to show only the minimum data points merchants need to identify and track an order. Details like menu items, quantity, price, and notes were moved to a dedicated order details page. This reduced clutter and allowed merchants to scan multiple orders at a glance.

We also overhauled the visual accessibility: replacing small, hard-to-read fonts with larger and more legible ones, and improving color contrasts throughout. Before launch, we ran a beta test with merchants across different business types and scales to validate the design under real orders and real conditions.

Outcome

Merchants responded positively. Feedback showed the new inbox helped them fulfill orders more accurately. We also saw a decline in help tickets related to order errors, a strong signal that merchants were reading and processing order information more reliably than before.

Learning points

The hardest part wasn't the design. It was getting the project prioritized. The product and business teams were initially reluctant because no core metric was visibly broken: orders were still coming in, fulfillment rates were still high. Making the case required reframing the question: merchants could fulfill orders, but at what cost to them? Advocating for merchant experience as a valid problem worth solving, even without a dramatic metric drop, was the real design challenge of this project.
