Making Compensation Request Easy for merchants

Company

GoTo (Gojek/GoFood Merchants)

Year

2022

Role

Product Designer

Team

PM, Engineers, Legal

Overview

When merchants experienced order issues — missing items, wrong deliveries, system errors — their only path to compensation was contacting customer support. This created long wait times, unclear resolution timelines, and significant frustration for merchants trying to run their businesses.

Problem

Merchants had no visibility into their compensation status and were fully dependent on support agents to initiate and resolve claims. Support teams were overloaded, and merchants often gave up on legitimate claims entirely.

Add your design screenshots here

My approach

I began with a research sprint — interviewing merchants and shadowing support agents to understand the full compensation lifecycle. We mapped every touchpoint from issue discovery to payment resolution, and identified the moments where merchants lost confidence in the system.

The design challenge was creating a self-serve flow that gave merchants enough control to feel empowered, while maintaining the guardrails GoFood needed to prevent abuse. I ran multiple rounds of concept testing with active merchants to validate the information architecture.

Add flow diagrams or wireframes here

Key design decisions

The core of the solution was a claim tracker with real-time status updates — giving merchants the visibility they were missing. Rather than a generic form, the flow was structured around order context, pulling in relevant order data automatically so merchants didn't have to re-explain what happened.

Helping Merchants Manage Orders in Peak-Hours

Company

GoTo (Gojek/GoFood Merchants)

Year

2023

Role

Product Designer

Team

PM, Engineers, UX Researcher, UX Writer

Overview

The order inbox was the most critical screen in the GoFood merchant app — the place where merchants see, accept, and manage every incoming order. Despite its importance, it hadn't been meaningfully redesigned in years, and merchants were struggling during high-volume periods.

Problem

Merchants were missing orders, struggling to prioritize, and losing time switching between statuses during peak hours. The interface treated all orders equally, with no visual differentiation for urgency or required action.

Add before/after screenshots here

Research & discovery

I visited merchant kitchens during the lunch rush to understand the real conditions — noisy, fast-moving, often with the phone mounted on the wall across the room. The insights fundamentally changed how I thought about visual hierarchy: tap targets, contrast, and information density needed to work at a glance from a distance.

Add research artifacts or user journey here

Solution

The redesign introduced a clear status-based tab structure, larger tap targets, color-coded urgency indicators, and a persistent action bar for the most common merchant actions. New orders now surface with clear visual prominence, and the interface gracefully handles the transition between empty, medium, and high-volume states.

Enable Merchants to Edit Ongoing Orders Seamlessly

Team

PM, Engineers, Data Analyst

Duration

3 months

Skills

UX Research, Interaction Design, Prototyping

Overview

When an ordered item is unavailable, merchants previously had only one option: cancel the entire order. This hurt merchants (lost revenue, lower ratings), customers (meal disrupted), and GoFood's platform metrics. Edit Order was designed to create a third path — modification.

Problem

Item unavailability was a significant driver of order cancellations. Merchants had no way to communicate alternatives or adjust orders — leading to the worst possible outcome for everyone involved.

Add screenshots of the edit order flow here

Design challenges

The core challenge was designing a flow that was fast enough for real kitchen conditions, while covering the complexity of order modifications — item removal, substitution, quantity changes, and the downstream effects on pricing and customer notification.

I worked closely with engineering and the customer experience team to define what changes required customer approval, which could be applied automatically, and how to communicate edits back to customers in a way that felt fair and transparent.

Add decision tree or system diagram here

Solution

The final design centered on an inline edit mode within the order detail view — keeping merchants in context rather than jumping to a separate editing screen. Unavailable items were highlighted immediately, and the summary panel updated in real time as changes were made, giving merchants confidence before confirming.
